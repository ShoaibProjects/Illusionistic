What are Auditory illusions?

What are Auditory illusions?

Auditory illusions are sound-based phenomena that trick the brain into hearing something that isn't there or interpreting sounds in misleading ways. These illusions occur due to the brain's processing of sound, which can be influenced by context, timing, frequency, and sensory integration, leading to perceptions of nonexistent sounds or altered interpretations.

McGurk effect

McGurk effect

At first it will sound 'BA' then 'FA' but its is 'BA' both times!

The McGurk effect is a perceptual phenomenon that demonstrates an interaction between hearing and vision in speech perception. The illusion occurs when the auditory component of one sound is paired with the visual component of another sound, leading to the perception of a third sound. The visual information a person gets from seeing a person speak changes the way they hear the sound.

Explanation

The McGurk effect is a perceptual phenomenon where conflicting visual lip movements and auditory sounds result in the perception of a third sound, highlighting the interaction between visual and auditory information in speech perception.

Yanny or Laurel

Yanny or Laurel

Whether you will hear "Yanny" or "Lourel"?

Yanny or Laurel is an auditory illusion that became popular in May 2018, in which a short audio recording of speech can be heard as one of two words. 53 percent of over 500,000 respondents to a Twitter poll reported hearing a man saying the word "Laurel", while 47 percent of people reported hearing a voice saying the name "Yanny".

Explanation

Analysis of the sound frequencies has confirmed that both sets of sounds are present in the mixed recording, but some users focus on the higher frequency sounds in "Yanny" and cannot seem to hear the lower sounds of the word "Laurel". When the audio clip is slowed to lower frequencies, the word "Yanny" is heard by more listeners, while faster playback loudens "Laurel".

Shepard tone

Shepard tone

The audio keeps getting faster or higher pitch but is just in loop!

A Shepard tone, named after Roger Shepard, is a sound consisting of a superposition of sine waves separated by octaves. When played with the bass pitch of the tone moving upward or downward, it is referred to as the Shepard scale. This creates the auditory illusion of a tone that seems to continually ascend or descend in pitch, yet which ultimately gets no higher or lower.

Explanation

As these tones cycle, the transitions between them are carefully managed to create the perception of a continuous, infinite rise or fall in pitch, even though the sequence repeats. This effect tricks the brain into perceiving a never-ending ascent or descent, similar to the visual illusion of a barbershop pole.

Flash-beep illusion.

Flash-beep illusion.

How many flashes did you count?

In this phenomenon, two brief flashes of light, when coupled with three beeps or tones, can make the flashes appear as if there are three distinct flashes. This occurs because the auditory cues influence the visual perception, causing the brain to group the flashes and sounds together in a way that creates the illusion of an additional flash.

Explanation

This illusion is caused by the way our brain integrates multisensory information. When two flashes of light are paired with three beeps, the auditory stimuli can lead to a perceptual misalignment, causing the brain to group the sounds and flashes together. This temporal binding makes it appear as though there are three flashes, even though only two are presented.

Talking piano

Talking piano

Pianos can talk?

The talking piano illusion is an auditory illusion where a sequence of piano notes is perceived as spoken words or phrases when played at a specific speed and pitch. This happens because the brain interprets the musical tones as speech sounds, especially when the notes are played in a rhythm and pattern similar to speech.

Explanation

The illusion is caused by the brain’s ability to interpret musical tones as speech when they are played in a rhythm and pattern that resembles the cadence and intonation of spoken language. The specific arrangement of pitch and timing tricks the auditory system into perceiving recognizable speech patterns, even though only piano notes are played.
