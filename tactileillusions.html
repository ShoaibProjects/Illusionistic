What are Tactile illusions?

What are Tactile illusions?

Tactile illusions are when the sense of touch is tricked into perceiving sensations that aren't actually there. This happens because the brain interprets conflicting or unusual sensory signals in a way that creates a false or altered perception of touch. These illusions demonstrate how the brain integrates touch information with other sensory inputs to shape our experience of physical sensations.

The Aristotle illusion

The Aristotle illusion

Cross your fingers, then touch a small object such as a pencil, and it feels like you are touching two pensils!

The Aristotle illusion is a tactile phenomenon that occurs when you cross your fingers and touch a small object, like a marble or a pen, between the tips. You may feel as though you are touching two separate objects instead of one.

Explanation

This illusion happens because the brain interprets the unusual finger positions as signals indicating the presence of two objects due to the unexpected tactile sensation on opposite sides of each finger.

Thermal grill illusion

Thermal grill illusion

When hand is pressed on mindly warm and cool grill bars, it feels like burning!

The illusion is created by an interlaced grill of bars which are warm, e.g. 40 °C, and cool, e.g. 20 °C. When someone presses a hand against the grill, they experience the illusion of burning heat. But if the person presses against only a cool bar, only coolness is experienced; if the person presses against only a warm bar, only warmth is experienced.

Explanation

This illusion is caused by the way the nervous system processes conflicting temperature signals, leading to the perception of pain without an actual harmful stimulus.

Rubber hand illusion

Rubber hand illusion

A person can conditioned to feeling that a rubber hand is his/her own hand!

In a experiment, subjects with normal brain function were positioned with their left hand hidden out of sight. They saw a lifelike rubber left hand in front of them. The experimenters stroked both the subjects hidden left hand and the visible rubber hand with a paintbrush. The experiment showed that if the two hands were stroked synchronously and in the same direction, the subjects began to experience the rubber hand as their own.

Explanation

This illusion is caused by the brain's integration of visual, tactile, and proprioceptive information, leading to a sense of ownership over the rubber hand when it is seen and felt being stroked in synchrony with the hidden real hand. This multisensory integration tricks the brain into perceiving the rubber hand as part of the body.

Cutaneous rabbit illusion

Cutaneous rabbit illusion

Tap on wrist twice and then elbow. It could feels like taps happens across the arm.

The cutaneous rabbit illusion is a tactile phenomenon in which a sequence of rapid taps applied to one area of the skin leads to the perception that the taps are spreading or "hopping" along the skin toward another area, even though no physical movement occurs between the points of contact. For example, if a series of taps is delivered to the wrist and then to the elbow, a person may feel as if the taps have traveled up their forearm like a hopping rabbit.

Explanation

This illusion occurs because the brain interprets the rapid sequence of taps as a continuous motion, filling in gaps with perceived sensations along the path.

Pinocchio illusion

Pinocchio illusion

Nose extension sensation illusion

Its a sensory illusion that can be triggered by irritation of various muscle groups through vibration in experiments, and causes a temporary disturbance of the depth sensitivity and thus of the sense of position of individual body parts. For example, people who touch their own nose while blindfolded can feel as if their nose is up to 30 cm long when vibrations are simultaneously triggered in the biceps of the same arm. This also works if the blindfolded person touches the nose of another person standing in front of them.

Explanation

The cause is that the vibrations disrupt the position of the arm, which signals to the brain that the arm should be extended more. This occurs because the brain tries to reconcile the conflicting sensory information, resulting in the perception of an elongating nose.
